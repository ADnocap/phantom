\begin{abstract}
Cross-sectional return prediction---ranking which assets will outperform their peers---is a cornerstone of quantitative investing, yet remains underexplored in cryptocurrency markets where distributional properties (heavy tails, regime shifts) demand more than point forecasts.
We present Phantom, a Transformer-based model that predicts full Student-$t$ distributions of cross-sectional \emph{relative} returns across 362 cryptocurrencies at horizons of 1--30 days, using only standard OHLCV price and volume features.
The model employs a patched encoder with cross-attention decoding for simultaneous multi-horizon prediction, trained on cross-sectionally demeaned returns to isolate idiosyncratic signal from shared market factors.
On 15 months of fully out-of-sample data (January 2025--March 2026), Phantom achieves a rank information coefficient (IC) of 0.124 at the 10-day horizon ($t = 8.93$, Newey-West), with a long-short portfolio Sharpe ratio of 11.84 net of 30 basis points transaction costs, a 74.7\% daily win rate, and perfect decile monotonicity.
Double-sorted portfolio analysis reveals that Phantom's signal is concentrated in high-volatility assets (IC$\,{=}\,$0.092, $t\,{=}\,$6.77) and is partially correlated with the low-volatility anomaly ($\rho\,{=}\,$0.63), yet combining both signals yields the highest IC of 0.157.
Through systematic ablation across eight model versions, we establish that the cross-sectional signal resides in daily OHLCV patterns, cannot be extracted from absolute returns, does not benefit from derivative market features (funding rates, order flow), and scales with the breadth of the asset universe---but not with higher-frequency data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The cryptocurrency market, with a combined capitalization exceeding \$2 trillion and continuous 24/7 trading across hundreds of liquid assets, presents a distinctive opportunity for cross-sectional return prediction.
Unlike directional forecasting---predicting whether an asset's price will rise or fall---cross-sectional prediction concerns the simpler but economically powerful task of \emph{ranking}: identifying which assets will outperform or underperform their peers over a given horizon.
Recent work has established that cross-sectional factors familiar from equity markets, including momentum~\cite{liu2022}, size, and volume, carry significant premia in cryptocurrency~\cite{fieberg2024}.
However, existing approaches rely exclusively on point forecasts, discarding the distributional information that is critical for position sizing, risk management, and tail-risk awareness in a market characterized by heavy tails, extreme volatility clustering, and frequent regime shifts.

This paper addresses a gap at the intersection of distributional forecasting and cross-sectional prediction.
While probabilistic time series models such as DeepAR and Temporal Fusion Transformers produce predictive distributions for individual assets, they are not designed for cross-sectional ranking.
Conversely, cross-sectional factor models~\cite{liu2022,fieberg2024} produce rankings but provide no uncertainty quantification.
Distributional foundation models such as JointFM~\cite{hackmann2026} have shown that Transformer architectures can recover complex distributional structure from time series, but have only been evaluated on synthetic data with no demonstrated cross-sectional signal on real financial assets.
We bridge these approaches.

We present \textbf{Phantom}, a Transformer-based model that predicts calibrated Student-$t$ distributions of cross-sectional \emph{relative} returns across 362 cryptocurrencies at horizons of 1--30 days simultaneously.
The architecture consists of a patched Transformer encoder that processes 120 days of 6-channel OHLCV features, a cross-attention decoder that queries all 30 horizons in a single forward pass, and a Student-$t$ output head with 3 parameters per horizon (location $\mu$, scale $\sigma$, degrees of freedom $\nu$).
The model is trained on cross-sectionally demeaned returns---each asset's return minus the same-day cross-sectional mean---which removes shared market factors and isolates the idiosyncratic signal that drives relative performance.

On 15 months of fully out-of-sample data (January 2025 to March 2026, 430 trading days), Phantom achieves a rank information coefficient (IC) of 0.124 at the 10-day horizon (Newey-West $t = 8.93$), increasing to 0.166 at 30 days.
A long-short portfolio that buys the top quintile and shorts the bottom quintile earns a Sharpe ratio of 11.84 after 30 basis points of transaction costs per trade, with a 74.7\% daily win rate, perfect decile return monotonicity (Spearman $\rho = 1.0$), and IC positive in all 15 out-of-sample months.
The strategy's breakeven transaction cost is 336 basis points, providing substantial headroom above typical exchange fees.

These results emerge from a systematic search over the design space, spanning eight model versions.
We show that (i)~synthetic pretraining on stochastic differential equations achieves oracle distributional accuracy but transfers zero signal to real data; (ii)~absolute returns are unpredictable from OHLCV features at any horizon, consistent with weak-form market efficiency; (iii)~\emph{relative} returns unlock cross-sectional signal concentrated in the cryptocurrency market; (iv)~derivative market features (funding rates, taker buy volume) provide no incremental IC; (v)~higher-frequency (4-hour) data degrades performance due to massive sample overlap; and (vi)~widening the asset universe from 60 to 362 cryptocurrencies doubles the Sharpe ratio.
Each negative result eliminates a plausible design choice and contributes to the understanding of where cross-sectional signal resides in cryptocurrency markets.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose Phantom, the first distributional forecasting model
    for cross-sectional cryptocurrency return prediction, producing
    calibrated Student-$t$ distributions at 30 horizons simultaneously.

    \item Through systematic ablation across eight model versions, we
    establish that (i) cross-sectional signal exists in daily OHLCV
    features, (ii) absolute returns are unpredictable from price/volume
    alone (consistent with weak-form efficiency), (iii) derivative market
    features (funding rates, taker buy ratios) provide no incremental
    signal, and (iv) higher-frequency (4-hour) data degrades performance
    due to sample overlap.

    \item We demonstrate that the signal scales with asset universe
    breadth (Sharpe doubles from 60 to 362 assets) and survives
    transaction costs up to 336 basis points per trade.

    \item We characterize the signal's relationship to the
    low-volatility anomaly via double-sorted portfolios: Phantom's
    predictive power concentrates in high-volatility assets
    (IC$\,{=}\,$0.092, $t\,{=}\,$6.77) where low-vol sorting alone
    provides no ranking, and combining both signals achieves the
    highest IC of 0.157.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Cross-Sectional Cryptocurrency Factors}

Liu, Tsyvinski, and Wu~\cite{liu2022} establish a three-factor model for cryptocurrency returns comprising market (CMKT), size (CSMB), and momentum (CMOM) factors, constructed analogously to the Fama-French framework for equities.
Momentum emerges as the dominant cross-sectional predictor, with weekly long-short returns and $t$-statistics well above~3, substantially exceeding the momentum premium observed in equity markets.
Their work demonstrates that standard asset pricing methodology transfers to the crypto asset class, though the factor zoo is far smaller than in equities.
Fieberg et al.~\cite{fieberg2024} extend this line of work by constructing CTREND, a composite trend factor that aggregates momentum and moving-average crossover signals across multiple lookback horizons.
CTREND subsumes any individual momentum measure and earns significant alpha over the three-factor model, demonstrating that multi-horizon trend aggregation is more informative than any single-horizon signal.

The low-volatility anomaly (low-volatility assets earn higher risk-adjusted returns than high-volatility ones) is well-documented in equities~\cite{ang2006} and has been observed in cryptocurrency markets, where the retail-dominated investor base and lottery-like preferences for high-volatility tokens provide a natural explanation.
Our double-sort analysis (Section~5.4) reveals substantial interaction between our model's signal and the low-volatility effect.

All existing cross-sectional crypto studies produce point forecasts: they rank assets by a scalar signal and evaluate via portfolio sorts.
None provides distributional predictions that quantify the \emph{uncertainty} of the ranking, which is essential for position sizing and risk management.

\subsection{Transformer-Based Time Series Forecasting}

Nie et al.~\cite{nie2023} introduce PatchTST, which segments time series into subseries-level patches before applying a Transformer encoder, reducing sequence length while capturing local semantic information.
Their finding that channel-independent processing outperforms channel mixing on standard benchmarks provides a foundation for our patched encoder design, though we depart from channel independence by jointly embedding all six OHLCV features within each patch, as cross-channel interactions (e.g., between returns and volume) are central to our prediction task.
Ansari et al.~\cite{ansari2024} propose Chronos, a time series foundation model that tokenizes real-valued observations into discrete bins and trains a T5-based language model on a large corpus of diverse time series, enabling zero-shot probabilistic forecasting across domains.
While both PatchTST and Chronos advance time series forecasting, neither is designed for cross-sectional ranking or the heavy-tailed distributional properties specific to financial returns.

\subsection{Distributional Foundation Models}

Most closely related to our work, Hackmann~\cite{hackmann2026} proposes JointFM, a foundation model for multivariate distributional time series forecasting.
JointFM uses Variable-Factored Attention to model both temporal and cross-variable dependencies and employs parametric distributional heads---multivariate Gaussian mixtures and multivariate Skewed Student-$t$ distributions---to produce full joint predictive distributions over multiple coupled targets.
Trained on synthetic data generated from stochastic differential equations (GBM, Merton jump-diffusion, Bates stochastic volatility, regime-switching), JointFM achieves strong distributional calibration as measured by energy distance~\cite{szekely2013}.
However, JointFM remains evaluated \emph{exclusively} on synthetic processes and does not address real financial data, leaving open the question of whether synthetic pretraining transfers to actual asset returns.
Our work answers this question directly: through systematic comparison of synthetic and real-data pretraining (Section~5.7), we find that synthetic SDE pretraining achieves oracle distributional accuracy on synthetic test data but transfers zero cross-sectional signal to real cryptocurrency markets.
Phantom extends the distributional forecasting paradigm to real cross-sectional prediction with demonstrated out-of-sample signal.

\subsection{Probabilistic Forecasting}

Distributional forecasting of financial returns has a long history, from GARCH-family models to deep learning approaches including DeepAR and Temporal Fusion Transformers.
We evaluate distributional quality using the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS), a strictly proper scoring rule~\cite{gneiting2007}:
\begin{equation}
\text{CRPS}(F, y) = \mathbb{E}_F\!\left[|X - y|\right] - \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_F\!\left[|X - X'|\right]
\label{eq:crps}
\end{equation}
where $X, X'$ are independent draws from the predictive distribution~$F$ and $y$ is the realized value.
Strict propriety ensures that the score is uniquely minimized when the forecaster reports the true conditional distribution, providing a principled training and evaluation objective.

We parameterize predictive distributions as Student-$t(\mu, \sigma, \nu)$ rather than Gaussian for two reasons: (i)~cryptocurrency returns exhibit substantial excess kurtosis even at multi-day horizons, and (ii)~the degrees-of-freedom parameter $\nu$ provides an adaptive, learnable measure of tail heaviness per asset and horizon.
Our finding that the model consistently pushes $\nu \to 2$ (the minimum for finite variance) confirms that heavy-tailed modeling is essential for this asset class and that Gaussian assumptions are inadequate.
